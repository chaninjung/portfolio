Research

Publications & Academic Work

IEEE Access — SCI 2023  ·  First Author
Counter-Rotating Hoop Stabilizer and SVR Control for Two-Wheels Vehicle Applications

The capstone design project (Dept. 1st place, 2021) built a PID-controlled reaction wheel balancer for a two-wheeled vehicle. That result became the foundation for this SCI paper — the entire system was redesigned: replaced PID with SVR (Support Vector Regression), and replaced the single reaction wheel with a counter-rotating hoop — two wheels spinning in opposite directions to achieve more stable, bidirectional balance control. SVR predicted the required PWM voltage at each tilt angle; hyperparameter optimization with RBF kernel achieved 98.2% correlation.

The Structure of Continuous Scientific Revolutions

Outstanding Thesis

Undergraduate philosophy thesis. Proposed a new interpretation of Thomas Kuhn's paradigm shift theory — arguing that scientific revolutions should be read as continuous, not discontinuous. Used SVR as a conceptual framework to model paradigm selection as a regression problem over the history of science.

  • Critically analyzed Kuhn, Popper, Carnap, and Jo In-rae's interpretations of scientific progress
  • Proposed an SVR-based schema where paradigms are weighted data points and the regression line represents the best available scientific judgment across time
  • Argued that transitivity of paradigm criteria implies a continuous structure — connecting scientific revolutions like links in a chain
  • Selected as outstanding graduation thesis; published in the university's philosophy journal

Background

The Story

I spent 4.5 years as an HVAC design engineer — drawing systems, reading construction codes, and learning the dense regulatory language of the Korean construction industry. That background isn't just history; it's the reason I could build AI tools that engineers actually trust.

While working, I went back to university at Ewha Womans University, studying Mechanical & Biomedical Engineering alongside Philosophy. The combination sounds unusual, but it shaped how I think about AI: engineering gave me the precision, philosophy gave me the question — what does it mean for a machine to actually understand what a person means?

That question led me to publish a first-author SCI paper in IEEE Access, applying machine learning to a control problem that classical methods couldn't solve cleanly. It was the moment AI stopped being a tool I used and became the field I wanted to work in.

Today I build production LLM systems. And on the side, I'm working on a personal project that sits right at the center of all of this: teaching AI to understand subjective musical language — because "make it warmer" doesn't have a lookup table.

I've been playing guitar for 20 years. I mix my own recordings. I travel solo. I learn by building.
